  100 Days to Die
>>>><><><><><><><<<<
\\\\\\||||||||//////

So let me get this straight. I'm going to die?

"Maybe."

He hangs in head low as he feels the crushing weight of the universe envelop his frail body. It's too soon. Especially for someone as young as him. Death was supposed to be impossible, just something he needed to worry about later in life. Now it's here, face to face and reeking of utter shit.

"I understand this is something that takes time to digest. But please understand there are steps that can still be taken. We have rigorous treatment procedures that have made huge impacts on people in the past."

Rigorous. Oh god. He can feel the needles, the chemo coursing through him and burning him brightly, trying to desperately save what's left of him. 

"Now we also need to remember this isn't something that affects just you. Any family members that you feel should know this must be informed as soon as possible. The more support we get, the better."

I haven't seen them in a while. My father is doing ok. So is mom I think. But I don't know much anymore about my older sister. 

"Did you want your sister to be informed?"

Unfazed and losing attention, his eyes focus on reality. Little details are picked up as he breathes. The dirt and soft creases on his sneakers. One shoelace is tied shorter than the other, but otherwise they've both lost their original shade of white. Now, flecks of brown and black populate them. He feels his lungs expand and contract, the graceful movements of his muscles, the feeling of life. He allows his ears to  carefully pay attention to the noises around him. The annoying doctor, the squeaking of his small metal swivel chair fighting against his weight. It's all around him. For the time being, he feels alive and tries to remember every detail of the feeling. He'll miss it shortly. It's amazing how little he appreciated being alive. 

"I suppose you need some time to think about all this. I know it's a lot to take in for anyone."

Yeah. Go out on a nice sunny day. Visit your doctor. Die.

"We can never see these things coming. The good thing is that we've caught it."

It's not much of a victory. He feels the thin paper doctors lay over their patient's beds. The faded blue leather underneath sneaks through in dark blotches that change shape as you press the paper with your hands. It's a cold clammy feel, and the paper rips gently underneath his finger nails.

"Right now, you'll doubt a lot of things."

Past life choices rifle through him. Regrets over every drug he took overwhelm him. His spine freezes up as he feels everything getting cold and faint. His knuckles turn white as his fingers press hard against the bed.

Am I going to die?

The doctor sighs and looks for an answer.

"There's not a clear answer for that right now. We still don't know a lot and the precautions we can take right now can only do so much."

Thinking the talking is offering some help, the doctor continues by throwing medical jargon around.

I don't get much of what you're saying. But I know I can understand it, I just can't seem to pay attention. It's almost surreal to know my fate.

"We do have on good thing going for us."

The small shred of good news perks his ears up. What could possibly be considered good news after all this bad news?

"You're still alive. You've still got time."

They stare at each other in silence.
